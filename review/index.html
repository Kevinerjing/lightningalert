Core Review

Must-Know Ideas From This Unit

Big Ideas

  • Static electricity is charge that builds up on the surface of an object.
  • Electrons move; protons stay in the nucleus. This is one of the most important test ideas.
  • Like charges repel and opposite charges attract.
  • A neutral object has the same number of protons and electrons.
  • An object becomes negative when it gains electrons.
  • An object becomes positive when it loses electrons.
  • Conductors let electrons move easily. Insulators do not.
  • Induction can cause a temporary separation of charge without touching.
  • Grounding lets extra electrons move to Earth or from Earth to remove charge buildup.

Most Likely Teacher Questions

  • Why does a charged balloon stick to a wall?
  • Why can a charged rod attract a neutral pith ball?
  • Which way do electrons move during charging by contact?
  • How do you read the electrostatic series?

  • Why does a person get a shock after walking on carpet?

Strong test answers usually include these words: electrons, attract, repel, neutral, temporary charge separation, contact, induction, and grounding.

Attract vs Repel

Situation Result
Positive + negative Attract
Positive + positive Repel
Negative + negative Repel
Charged + neutral Often attract because of induction

Temporary vs Permanent Charge

Type What Happens
Induced temporary charge Charges separate only while the charged object is nearby
Net charge after friction/contact The object actually gains or loses electrons
Common mistake: saying a positive rod gives positive charges to another object. In this unit, the safe answer is usually: electrons move, not positive charges.
Electrostatic Series

How To Read The Electrostatic Series

Reading Rule

  • The material with the stronger attraction for electrons pulls electrons from the other material.
  • The material that gains electrons becomes negative.
  • The material that loses electrons becomes positive.
  • The farther apart two materials are on the series, the stronger the charging effect is often expected to be.

Example

If vinyl attracts electrons more strongly than silk, then electrons move from silk to vinyl. Vinyl becomes negative. Silk becomes positive. Because the charges are opposite, the two materials will attract.

Real Life

Real-Life Examples You Can Use In Short Answers

Balloon Sticking to a Wall

Rubbing the balloon on hair gives the balloon extra electrons, so it becomes negative. When it comes near the wall, electrons in the wall are pushed slightly away. The near side of the wall becomes relatively positive, so the balloon is attracted.

Shock After Walking on Carpet

Friction moves electrons between your shoes, the carpet, and your body. When you touch a metal doorknob, the charge discharges quickly through one point, which is why you feel a small shock.

Dust on a TV or Computer Screen

A charged screen can attract neutral dust particles by induction. The charges in the dust shift slightly, and the dust is pulled toward the screen.

Water Bending Toward a Charged Rod

Water molecules have uneven charge distribution. A charged rod makes the charges in the water line up more, so the stream is attracted and bends.

Why Hair Stands Up

When hairs get the same charge, they repel each other. That is why they spread apart and stand up after static charging.

Gasoline Truck Grounding Chain

A fuel truck can build up static charge while moving and while fuel flows. The grounding chain lets extra charge move safely to Earth so a spark does not form. This matters because a spark near gasoline vapour could start a fire or explosion.

Writing Help

Short Answer Formula That Sounds Like a Strong Science Answer

Formula 1: name the charge + explain electron movement + state the result
Formula 2: identify the charging method + say whether touching happens + explain why attraction or repulsion occurs
Formula 3: use the electrostatic series + say who gains electrons + say who becomes positive/negative

Model Answer: Balloon and Wall

A balloon rubbed on hair gains electrons and becomes negatively charged. When it comes near a neutral wall, the electrons in the wall shift slightly away, so the near surface becomes relatively positive. Because opposite charges attract, the balloon sticks to the wall.

Model Answer: Gas Truck Chain

The chain grounds the truck. Extra electrons can move to Earth, which prevents a buildup of static charge. This reduces the chance of a spark, and that is important because gasoline vapour is flammable.
