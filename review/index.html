Chapter 2: Chemical Reactions

Clean one-page review | Conservation of mass | Balancing equations | Reaction types | Two mistake-focused practice tests

1. Core Concepts

Law of Conservation of Mass

The total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products. Atoms are not created or destroyed; they are rearranged.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

Common mistake: Balancing does not create new atoms. It only makes the number of each atom equal on both sides.

State Symbols

SymbolMeaning
(s)solid
(l)liquid / molten
(g)gas
(aq)aqueous: dissolved in water
Common mistake: NaCl(aq) is not liquid salt. It means salt dissolved in water.

2. Balancing Chemical Equations

Golden rule: Change coefficients only. Never change subscripts.

Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃

Balanced:

4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃

RuleWhy it matters
Only add coefficientsChanging subscripts changes the substance.
Balance non-H and non-O firstHydrogen and oxygen often appear in many places, so they are usually easier to finish last.
Treat unchanged polyatomic ions as one unitExample: keep SO₄ together if it appears unchanged on both sides.
Use simplest whole-number coefficientsDo not leave fractions or reducible coefficients.
If impossible, check formulasThe skeleton equation may be written incorrectly.

4. Complete vs. Incomplete Combustion

Complete Combustion

Occurs when there is enough oxygen.

Hydrocarbon products: CO₂ + H₂O + energy

Flame: usually blue, less smoke.

Incomplete Combustion

Occurs when oxygen is limited.

Possible products: CO₂, CO, H₂O, C(s), energy

Flame: yellow/orange and smokier.

Important: Carbon monoxide, CO, is colourless, odourless, tasteless, and dangerous.

5. High-Frequency Mistakes

  1. Changing subscripts instead of coefficients.
  2. Forgetting that some elements are diatomic: H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂.
  3. Thinking (aq) means liquid. It means dissolved in water.
  4. Breaking polyatomic ions when they should be treated as a unit.
  5. Writing wrong ionic formulas before balancing, such as using AgCrO₄ instead of Ag₂CrO₄.
  6. Calling every reaction with oxygen “combustion.” Oxygen may appear in other reaction types too.

6. Complete Workflow: From English Description to Final Equation

Use this full workflow every time:

English sentence → word equation → skeleton equation → balanced equation → reaction type

StepWhat to doExample
1. Read the sentenceFind the reactants and products.Magnesium reacts with oxygen to form magnesium oxide.
2. Write a word equationUse words first before formulas.magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide
3. Write correct formulasUse symbols, charges, crisscrossing, and diatomic elements.Mg + O₂ → MgO
4. BalanceAdd coefficients only. Never change subscripts.2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
5. ClassifyLook at the pattern.Synthesis
Big idea: Many Grade 10 students think they are bad at balancing, but the real mistake often happens earlier: they write the wrong chemical formula before balancing.

7. Writing Chemical Formulas Cheat Sheet

When these elements appear alone, write them as two atoms:

H₂, O₂, N₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂

Common trap: oxygen gas is O₂, but oxide inside a compound is usually O²⁻. Example: magnesium oxide is MgO, not MgO₂.

Ontario Grade 10 Chemistry: Most Common Lost Marks

  1. Writing O instead of O₂ when oxygen appears alone.
  2. Changing subscripts to balance, such as changing H₂O into H₂O₂.
  3. Forgetting brackets for polyatomic ions: Ca(NO₃)₂, not CaNO₃₂.
  4. Mixing up similar ions: sulfide S²⁻, sulfate SO₄²⁻, sulfite SO₃²⁻.
  5. Ignoring Roman numerals: iron(II) and iron(III) make different compounds.
  6. Writing products before checking reaction type patterns.
  7. Forgetting that complete combustion of hydrocarbons produces CO₂ and H₂O.
  8. Balancing before checking whether the skeleton equation is chemically correct.

Practice Test 1: Easy-to-Miss Questions

Try these first without looking at the answers.

  1. Balance: __K₂O → __K + __O₂
  2. Balance: __AlCl₃ + __Na₂CO₃ → __Al₂(CO₃)₃ + __NaCl
  3. Write and balance: magnesium reacts with oxygen.
  4. Write and balance the complete combustion of propane, C₃H₈.
  5. Predict products and balance: Fe + Pb(NO₃)₂ →
  6. Predict products and balance: Cl₂ + NaI →
  7. Classify: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
  8. Classify: AgNO₃ + NaCl → NaNO₃ + AgCl
  9. What does CuSO₄(aq) mean?
  10. Explain why Fe + O₃ → Fe₂O₃ is not the normal skeleton equation for iron reacting with oxygen.
Answers and analysis: Test 1
  1. 2K₂O → 4K + O₂
    Oxygen is diatomic as O₂, so two K₂O units are needed.
  2. 2AlCl₃ + 3Na₂CO₃ → Al₂(CO₃)₃ + 6NaCl
    Treat CO₃ as a unit. Then balance Al, CO₃, Na, and Cl.
  3. 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
    Oxygen is O₂, not O.
  4. C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
    Balance C first, H second, O last.
  5. Fe + Pb(NO₃)₂ → Fe(NO₃)₂ + Pb
    Single displacement. Use the common charge Fe²⁺ unless otherwise stated.
  6. Cl₂ + 2NaI → 2NaCl + I₂
    A nonmetal replaces another nonmetal. Iodine is diatomic.
  7. Decomposition
    One reactant breaks into multiple products.
  8. Double displacement
    The ions exchange partners; AgCl is a precipitate.
  9. Copper(II) sulfate dissolved in water.
    Aqueous means dissolved in water.
  10. Oxygen gas is normally O₂, not O₃.
    O₃ is ozone, a different substance. Do not change chemical formulas just to balance.

Practice Test 2: More Tricky Mixed Questions

  1. Balance: __N₂O₅ → __N₂O₄ + __O₂
  2. Write and balance: methane burns completely in oxygen.
  3. Write and balance: zinc metal reacts with silver nitrate solution to produce zinc nitrate and silver metal.
  4. Predict products and balance: Li + H₂O →
  5. Predict products and balance: CaBr₂ + KOH →
  6. Write and balance: silver nitrate + sodium phosphate → silver phosphate precipitate + sodium nitrate.
  7. Classify: P₄ + 5O₂ → P₄O₁₀
  8. Classify: K₂O + H₂O → 2KOH
  9. Which is more dangerous in incomplete combustion: CO or CO₂? Why?
  10. A student writes AgNO₃ + KCrO₄ → AgCrO₄ + KNO₃. What is the formula mistake?
Answers and analysis: Test 2
  1. 2N₂O₅ → 2N₂O₄ + O₂
    Nitrogen is already balanced when both nitrogen compounds have coefficient 2. Oxygen becomes 10 on each side.
  2. CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
    Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon forms CO₂ and H₂O.
  3. Zn + 2AgNO₃ → Zn(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag
    Zinc forms Zn²⁺, so zinc nitrate is Zn(NO₃)₂.
  4. 2Li + 2H₂O → 2LiOH + H₂
    Metal + water often forms a metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas.
  5. CaBr₂ + 2KOH → Ca(OH)₂ + 2KBr
    Double displacement. Calcium is 2+, hydroxide is 1−, so Ca(OH)₂.
  6. 3AgNO₃ + Na₃PO₄ → Ag₃PO₄ + 3NaNO₃
    Phosphate is PO₄³⁻, silver is Ag⁺, so silver phosphate is Ag₃PO₄.
  7. Combustion
    Phosphorus reacts with oxygen and releases energy.
  8. Synthesis
    Two reactants combine to form one product.
  9. CO is more dangerous.
    Carbon monoxide reduces the blood’s ability to carry oxygen and is hard to detect because it is colourless and odourless.
  10. Silver chromate should be Ag₂CrO₄, not AgCrO₄.
    Ag is +1 and CrO₄ is −2, so two Ag⁺ ions are needed.

Homework-Specific High-Risk Points

These are the parts of Class 2 Homework that are most likely to cost marks even when the student understands the basic idea.

1. Dissolved vs. Precipitate

For AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq):

Reactants: AgNO₃(aq) and NaCl(aq)

Products: AgCl(s) and NaNO₃(aq)

Dissolved compounds: silver nitrate, sodium chloride, sodium nitrate.

Precipitate: silver chloride.

Trap: (aq) means dissolved in water; (s) is the solid precipitate.

2. Conservation of Mass Calculation

Propane + container = 61.8 g; container only = 50.0 g.

So propane mass = 61.8 - 50.0 = 11.8 g.

Products + container = 104.8 g, so products mass = 104.8 - 50.0 = 54.8 g.

Oxygen consumed = products - propane = 54.8 - 11.8 = 43.0 g O₂.

Trap: subtract the container first, or subtract it consistently. Do not use 104.8 g as product mass.

Hydrogen Peroxide

2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g); classification: decomposition.

(aq) means dissolved in water; (l) means pure liquid. As it reacts, bubbles of oxygen gas should appear. The reaction is complete when bubbling stops.

Quick Exam Strategy

  1. First write the correct chemical formulas.
  2. Then balance using coefficients only.
  3. Check every atom on both sides.
  4. Reduce coefficients to the simplest whole numbers.
  5. Classify by pattern, not by guessing from the name.

Chapter 3: Acids, Bases, and pH

Arrhenius acids and bases | Neutralization | Indicators | pH scale | Acid deposition | Homework traps

1. Core Concepts

Arrhenius Acids and Bases

Acid: produces H⁺ in water. Example: HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻.

Base: produces OH⁻ in water. Example: NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻.

Trap: a compound can be basic without ending in OH, such as hydrogen carbonate, because it can produce OH⁻ in water.

2. Neutralization and Acid Reactions

Neutralization

Acid + base → ionic salt + water.

HCl(aq) + LiOH(aq) → LiCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

Method: combine H⁺ and OH⁻ into water, then pair the remaining ions to make the salt.

Acids with Carbonates

Acid + carbonate → salt + carbon dioxide + water.

H₂SO₄ + CaCO₃ → CaSO₄ + CO₂ + H₂O

Trap: carbonate questions often ask for an observation: bubbles of CO₂.

pH Scale

pH < 7 acidic; pH = 7 neutral; pH > 7 basic.

The pH scale is logarithmic: a difference of 1 pH unit means a 10 times change.

Example: pH 2.3 to 6.7 is 10⁴⋅⁴ ≈ 25,000 times less acidic, so it became about 25,000 times more basic.

4. Acid Deposition and Applications

TopicWhat to remember
Natural rainAbout pH 5.6 because dissolved CO₂ forms carbonic acid.
Acid depositionBelow pH 5.6; caused by sulfur oxides and nitrogen oxides from fossil fuels.
EffectsDamages plants, leaches soil minerals, harms aquatic life, and damages buildings.
Pool pHGood range is about 7.2-7.8. If pH is 8.2, water is basic, cloudy, and chlorine works less well; add acid such as HCl to lower pH.

Homework-Specific High-Risk Points

Q1-Q2: Acid, Base, or Neutral

Acids: HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HI, H₃PO₄, HBr.

Bases: KOH, Ba(OH)₂, LiOH, KHCO₃, Ca(HCO₃)₂, Fe(OH)₃.

Q5: Universal Indicator Bottles

A is yellow, B is blue, C is peach.

Ammonia: B, because blue indicates a base.

Vinegar: A, because yellow indicates an acid near pH 6-8 on the provided chart.

Third bottle: C is not uniquely known; it is acidic, but many acidic cleaning fluids could give peach.

Safety: do not pour an unknown cleaning fluid down the drain just from indicator colour.

1. Cell Theory and Cell Types

Cell Theory

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the simplest unit that carries out life processes.
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

3. Cell Division and Cell Cycle

Why Cells Divide

  1. Reproduction
  2. Growth
  3. Repair

Cells divide instead of just getting bigger because large cells have lower surface area relative to volume, making diffusion and osmosis less efficient.

Cell Cycle Order

Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis

StageKey clue

ProphaseNuclear membrane dissolves; chromosomes become visible.
MetaphaseChromosomes line up in the middle.
AnaphaseSister chromatids separate.
TelophaseNuclear membranes reform.
CytokinesisCell splits into two daughter cells.

4. Cancer, Stem Cells, and Tissues

Cancer

Cancer is uncontrolled cell growth and division caused by DNA mutations. It may start as a tumour and can spread by metastasis.

Prevention and early detection: avoid smoking, use sun protection, maintain healthy habits, know family history, get screened.

Stem Cells

Embryonic stem cells: can become any cell type.

Adult stem cells: found in the body and usually become a limited range of cell types.
