History

Short Timeline

c. 600 BC - Thales of Miletus He noticed that rubbed amber attracted light objects. This was an early observation of static electricity and helped start the long path toward charge detection.
1600 - William Gilbert Gilbert invented the earliest electroscope, the versorium, a pivoting needle that turned when a charged object was nearby.
1754 - John Canton Canton developed the pith-ball electroscope, which made charge effects easier to observe using light suspended balls.
1787 - Abraham Bennet Bennet introduced the gold-leaf electroscope, a more sensitive design that made small charge changes easier to detect.

Types

Types of Electroscope

Pith-Ball Electroscope

Uses one or two very light balls on thread. A nearby charged object makes the balls move, so it is useful for showing attraction, repulsion, and charge transfer in a simple way.

Gold-Leaf Electroscope

Uses thin metal leaves inside a case. When charge reaches the leaves, they separate. It is more sensitive than the pith-ball electroscope and is the classic school model.

Practice Matrix

Rod Charge x Touching Matrix

1. Positive Rod, No Touching

Show Answer

Electrons move up toward the knob. The leaves lose electrons, become positive, and separate. This is induction because there is no touching.

2. Negative Rod, No Touching

Show Answer

Electrons are pushed down into the leaves. The leaves become negative and separate. This is also induction because the rod is only nearby.

3. Positive Rod, Touching

Show Answer

Electrons move from the electroscope into the positive rod. The electroscope is left with a net positive charge, so the leaves separate. This is charging by contact.

4. Negative Rod, Touching

Show Answer

Electrons move from the negative rod into the electroscope. The electroscope gains a net negative charge, so the leaves separate. This is charging by contact.

Fast Rules

What To Remember

Contact vs Grounding

Situation What electrons move between Best label
A charged rod touches the electroscope The rod and the electroscope Contact / conduction
A finger touches the electroscope The electroscope and the person, often effectively Earth in classroom questions Usually grounding
A wire connects the electroscope to Earth The electroscope and Earth Grounding

Important reminder: not every touching case is grounding. Touching a charged rod is usually conduction, not grounding.

Induction

No touching. Charges shift inside the electroscope. The total charge of the electroscope can stay neutral overall.

Contact

Touching happens. Electrons transfer between the rod and the electroscope, so the electroscope ends up with a net charge.

Positive Rod

It attracts electrons toward itself. If it touches, electrons leave the electroscope and the electroscope becomes positive.

Negative Rod

It pushes electrons away. If it touches, electrons enter the electroscope and the electroscope becomes negative.
