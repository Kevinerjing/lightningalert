1. Cell Theory and Cell Types

Cell Theory

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the simplest unit that carries out life processes.
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Prokaryote vs. Eukaryote

ProkaryoteEukaryote
No nucleusHas nucleus
No membrane-bound organellesHas membrane-bound organelles
Bacteria and archaeaPlants, animals, fungi, protists

3. Cell Division and Cell Cycle

Why Cells Divide

  1. Reproduction
  2. Growth
  3. Repair

Cells divide instead of just getting bigger because large cells have lower surface area relative to volume, making diffusion and osmosis less efficient.

Cell Cycle Order

Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis

StageKey clue
InterphaseCell grows and copies DNA.
ProphaseNuclear membrane dissolves; chromosomes become visible.
MetaphaseChromosomes line up in the middle.
AnaphaseSister chromatids separate.
TelophaseNuclear membranes reform.
CytokinesisCell splits into two daughter cells.

4. Cancer, Stem Cells, and Tissues

Cancer

Cancer is uncontrolled cell growth and division caused by DNA mutations. It may start as a tumour and can spread by metastasis.

Prevention and early detection: avoid smoking, use sun protection, maintain healthy habits, know family history, get screened.

Stem Cells

Embryonic stem cells: can become any cell type.

Adult stem cells: found in the body and usually become a limited range of cell types.

Tissue Types

TissueStructure / clueFunction
EpithelialThin sheets covering surfaces and lining organsProtection; reduces water loss
ConnectiveCells in matrix; examples include bone, blood, fatSupport, protection, connection, transport
MuscleCells with actin and myosin that contractMovement
NerveNeurons and supporting cellsTransmit electrical signals

5. Lesson Checkpoints and Analogies

Heart Muscle vs. Liver Cell

The heart muscle cell should have more mitochondria because it contracts constantly and needs a large supply of usable energy from cellular respiration.

Test wording: connect mitochondria to energy demand, not just "the picture has more M labels."

6. Cancer, Treatment, and Stem Cell Details

Healthy Cell Checkpoints

A cell should not divide if surrounding cells signal it to stop, nutrients are too low, DNA has not been copied, or DNA is damaged.

If damage cannot be repaired, the cell can undergo programmed cell death, called apoptosis.

What Causes Cancer?

Cancer can start when DNA mutations allow rapid growth, prevent cells from stopping division, or cause errors when repairing DNA.

Mutations can be inherited or caused by environmental factors such as radiation, viruses, and carcinogens.

Cancer Stages

StageMeaning
Stage 0Cancer cells remain in place and form a tumour.
Stage ISmall tumour; not grown deeply into nearby tissue and not spread to lymph nodes.
Stage II / IIILarger tumours have grown deeper and may have spread to lymph nodes.
Stage IVCancer has spread to other organs or body parts; this is metastasis.

Colorectal Cancer 5-Year Survival Rates

StageSurvival rate from lesson
I94%
II82%
III67%
IV11%
Big idea: screening matters because earlier stages are usually more treatable.

Treatments and Prevention

Surgery: physically removes a tumour.

Chemotherapy: uses chemicals or drugs to kill cancer cells.

Radiation therapy: uses focused radiation beams to target cancer cells.

Prevention: live smoke-free, wear sun protection, maintain healthy body weight, get vaccinated, check family history, and get screened.

Stem Cell Transplants

Stem cell transplants replace stem cells from a donor when a patient's stem cells or bone marrow have been damaged by disease, chemotherapy, or radiation therapy.

SourceWhat to remember
Bone marrowStem cells can be collected directly from marrow.
Peripheral bloodStem cells can be collected from circulating blood.
Umbilical cordCord blood can provide transplant stem cells.

7. Hierarchy of an Organism

cell -> tissue -> organ -> organ system -> organism

LevelDefinitionExample thinking
CellThe smallest functioning unit of an organism.Muscle cell, nerve cell, epithelial cell.
TissueA group of cells that perform a similar, limited function.Muscle tissue contracts; nerve tissue sends signals.
OrganA structure made of different tissues that perform a complex function.The heart contains muscle, connective, epithelial, and nerve tissue.
Organ systemOne or more organs working together for a vital body function.Circulatory system, digestive system, nervous system.
OrganismThe complete living thing.A human, plant, fungus, or bacterium.

Practice Test 1: Easy-to-Miss Questions

Try these first without looking at the answers.

  1. List the three parts of cell theory.
  2. State two obvious differences between prokaryotes and eukaryotes.
  3. State three major differences between plant and animal cells.
  4. Why do plant cells still need mitochondria if they have chloroplasts?
  5. Which organelle stores DNA and controls cell activities?
  6. Which organelle makes proteins?
  7. Which organelle packages and processes materials?
  8. Which organelle should be extra common in a heart muscle cell? Explain why.
  9. Why do cells divide instead of only getting bigger?
  10. Put these in order: anaphase, interphase, cytokinesis, metaphase, prophase, telophase.
  11. What is cytokinesis?
Answers and analysis: Test 1
  1. All living things are made of cells; the cell is the basic unit of life; all cells come from pre-existing cells.
  2. Prokaryotes have no nucleus and no membrane-bound organelles; eukaryotes have both.
  3. Plant cells have a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole.
  4. Chloroplasts make glucose, but mitochondria release usable energy from glucose.
  5. Nucleus.
  6. Ribosome.
  7. Golgi body.
  8. Mitochondria, because heart muscle needs lots of usable energy for constant contraction.
  9. Large cells have less surface area relative to volume, so diffusion and osmosis become less efficient.
  10. Interphase → Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis.
  11. The division of the cell into two genetically identical daughter cells.

Practice Test 2: More Tricky Mixed Questions

  1. A cell is growing and copying its DNA. Which stage is it in?
  2. The nuclear membrane dissolves and chromosomes become visible. Which stage is this?
  3. Chromosomes line up in the middle of the cell. Which stage is this?
  4. Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite ends. Which stage is this?
  5. A new cell wall or division plate begins to form. Which stage is this?
  6. How are cancer cells different from normal cells?
  7. Why can early cancer be easy to overlook?
  8. Compare embryonic stem cells and adult stem cells.
  9. Match tissue to function: epithelial, connective, muscle, nerve.
  10. Why is blood considered connective tissue?
  11. Put the organism hierarchy in order from smallest to largest.
  12. Name two ways cancer can be treated and one way cancer risk can be reduced.
Answers and analysis: Test 2
  1. Interphase.
    The cell grows and copies DNA before mitosis.
  2. Prophase.
  3. Metaphase.
  4. Anaphase.
  5. Cytokinesis.
    A division plate is especially associated with plant-cell cytokinesis.
  6. Cancer cells ignore normal checkpoints and divide uncontrollably; they may form tumours or spread.
  7. Early tumours may be small, localized, and cause few symptoms.
  8. Embryonic stem cells can become almost any cell type; adult stem cells are more limited.
  9. Epithelial: covers/lines and protects; connective: support/transport/connect; muscle: contracts for movement; nerve: transmits signals.
  10. Blood has cells suspended in plasma and connects body systems by transporting gases, nutrients, wastes, and signals.
  11. Cell -> tissue -> organ -> organ system -> organism.
  12. Treatments include surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation therapy. Risk can be reduced with choices such as living smoke-free, sun protection, vaccination, screening, and checking family history.
