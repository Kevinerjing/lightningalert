1. What This Page Covers

Lesson Targets

  1. Identify Arrhenius acids and bases.
  2. Name common acids and bases.
  3. Predict acid-metal, acid-carbonate, and neutralization reactions.
  4. Use acid-base indicators and the pH scale.
  5. Explain practical pH examples and acid deposition.

Fast Strategy

  1. Look for H+ production in water for acids.
  2. Look for OH- production in water for bases.
  3. For neutralization, combine H+ and OH- into water, then pair the remaining ions as the salt.

2. Arrhenius Acids and Bases

Acids

An Arrhenius acid produces H+ when dissolved in water.

HCl + H2O → H3O+ + Cl-

  1. Conduct electricity.
  2. Taste sour.
  3. Turn blue litmus red.
  4. Neutralize bases.

Bases

An Arrhenius base produces OH- when dissolved in water.

NaOH → Na+ + OH-

HCO3- + H2O → H2CO3 + OH-

  1. Conduct electricity.
  2. Taste bitter.
  3. Turn red litmus blue.
  4. Neutralize acids.

pH Scale

pH stands for "power of hydrogen" and measures acidity.

pH < 7 acidic

pH = 7 neutral

pH > 7 basic

The pH scale is logarithmic: 1 pH unit means a 10 times change.

pH Change Example

A substance changes from pH 2.3 to pH 6.7. Difference = 6.7 - 2.3 = 4.4. The change is 104.4, which is about 25,000 times more basic.

5. Applications of pH

TopicWhat to remember
AgricultureLegumes grow in slightly basic soil; corn prefers mildly acidic soil; potatoes grow best below pH 5.
Acid leachingAcid can remove basic heavy-metal contaminants from soil.
PhytoremediationPlants absorb toxins; sunflowers absorb toxic metals and radioactive elements; aspen trees remove lead from water.
Consumer productsBleach and oven cleaner are very basic; toilet bowl cleaner and battery acid are very acidic. Corrosive products need gloves and ventilation.
Swimming poolsIdeal pH is 7.2-7.8. If pH is above 8, water becomes cloudy and chlorine disinfects poorly. Add muriatic acid, HCl(aq), to lower pH; add Na2CO3(aq) to raise pH.

6. Acid Deposition

Sources

Rainwater is naturally slightly acidic, about pH 5.6, because dissolved carbon dioxide forms carbonic acid.

Acid deposition is below pH 5.6 and comes from air pollutants that acidify soil and water.

Sulfur oxides: from burning fossil fuels.

SO2 + H2O → H2SO3

SO3 + H2O → H2SO4

Nitrogen oxides: from burning fossil fuels.

2NO2 + H2O → HNO2 + HNO3

Effects

  1. Damages forests and slows plant growth.
  2. Injures leaves and blocks stomata for gas exchange.
  3. Leaches minerals from soil.
  4. Reduces aquatic life.
  5. Releases toxic Al3+ from rocks, which can interfere with fish gills.
  6. Nitrogen compounds can cause algal blooms and eutrophication.
  7. Damages buildings and structures.

7. Homework High-Risk Answers

Q1-Q2: Acid, Base, or Neutral

Acids: HNO3, H2SO4, HI, H3PO4, HBr.

Bases: KOH, Ba(OH)2, LiOH, KHCO3, Ca(HCO3)2, Fe(OH)3.

Neutral: KNO3.

Q5: Universal Indicator Bottles

Ammonia: B, because blue indicates a base.

Vinegar: A, because yellow indicates acid on the provided chart.

Third bottle: C is acidic, but its exact identity is not known from colour alone.

Safety: do not dispose of an unknown cleaning fluid down the drain based only on indicator colour.

  1. Write the reaction for sulfuric acid with calcium carbonate.
  2. A pH changes from 13.2 to 8.5. How many times did the concentration change?
  3. Why can lemon water reduce fishy seafood odour?
  4. Which lake resists acidification better: limestone or granite? Explain.
  5. A solution turns phenolphthalein pink. Acidic or basic?
Answers
  1. H2SO4 + CaCO3 → CaSO4 + CO2 + H2O
  2. Difference = 4.7 pH units, so about 104.7, or about 50,000 times.
  3. Lemon water is acidic and neutralizes basic amines.
  4. Limestone, because CaCO3 neutralizes acid.
  5. Basic.
