What A Teacher Usually Wants To Know
Element Heroes is a classroom-friendly chemistry card game. Students place substances, prepare reaction conditions, trigger attacks or reactions, and connect what happens in the game to real science ideas such as combustion, oxidation, ionic bonding, corrosion, conductivity, and reactivity.

Teacher Checklist
The key questions this page answers for classroom use.

What are students learning?

Reactants, products, chemical equations, signs of chemical change, ionic bonding, oxidation, corrosion, and reactivity.

How accurate is it?

Many cards model real chemistry directly. Some cards are simplified gameplay abstractions and should be described honestly.

How can I use it?

As a review game, discussion prompt, partner task, or consolidation activity after instruction.

How Students Play
The main gameplay loop and the science thinking behind it.
Scenario 1

Place a substance on the field

Students play a substance card such as oxygen, sulfur, water, iron, sodium, or chlorine.

Teacher link: elements, compounds, properties of matter, reactants prepared before a reaction.
Scenario 2

Check whether a reaction is possible

Students learn that a reaction card only works if the correct required cards are already present.

Teacher link: reactants and products, conditions for reaction, predicting outcomes from available substances.
Scenario 3

Use an attack when a reaction is not ready

Students still make strategic choices based on setup and status effects even without a full reaction.

Teacher link: properties and conditions still matter even when no new product is formed.
Scenario 4

Build a combo across multiple turns

Students place reactants now so a stronger move becomes possible later.

Teacher link: planning, limiting reagents, and understanding that chemical change often depends on setup.
Scenario 5

Vote for the next Element Hero

Students propose and vote on a new Element Hero and its chemical reaction ability using an element or compound of their choice.

Teacher link: creativity, chemical properties, reactions, and scientific reasoning.

Reaction Scenarios And Curriculum Links
Each major play scenario with the chemistry idea it supports.
Combustion

Sulfur + Oxygen

Students trigger a combustion-style reaction after placing sulfur and oxygen.

Teacher link: combustion, exothermic change, evidence of reaction, sulfur dioxide formation, blue flame observation.
Rust

Iron + Oxygen

Students connect iron and oxygen to corrosion and ongoing weakening.

Teacher link: oxidation, rusting, corrosion, role of water in speeding up rust formation.
Explosion

Hydrogen + Oxygen

A high-damage reaction models a rapid, energy-releasing combination.

Teacher link: 2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O, exothermic reactions, energy release, hydrogen combustion.
Salt Formation

Sodium + Chlorine

Students see a reaction that models electron transfer and the formation of a stable ionic compound.

Teacher link: 2Na + Cl2 -> 2NaCl, ionic bonding, electron transfer, Na+ and Cl-, attraction of opposite charges.
Alkali Reaction

Potassium + Water

This reaction shows a very reactive metal producing a strong effect.

Teacher link: 2K + 2H2O -> 2KOH + H2, metal-water reactivity, hydrogen release, heat production.
Lime Formation

Calcium + Water

Students see a material-forming reaction that also changes resources in the model.

Teacher link: calcium reactivity, product formation, energy and resource modeling in scientific simulations.
Steam Burst

Water + Oxygen

This is a simplified game combo rather than a single textbook equation.

Teacher link: discuss models versus exact chemistry, water vapor, and why games sometimes simplify science.
Status Effects

Wet and Corroded

Statuses carry scientific ideas forward into later turns instead of ending at one move.

Teacher link: corrosion over time, conductivity, and how conditions change later outcomes.
Utility And Strategy Scenarios
Important non-reaction moments that still support science thinking.
Utility

Catalyst and support cards

Students use support cards to gain resources, heal, or protect themselves.

Teacher link: catalysts as a classroom model for helping processes happen more effectively; energy here is a gameplay system.
Field Choice

Managing only 3 field slots

Students cannot place everything, so they must choose which substances matter most.

Teacher link: selecting reagents, planning reactions, and comparing efficient versus inefficient setups.
Conductivity

Lightning on a Wet target

Students are rewarded for using one scientific condition to strengthen another move.

Teacher link: conductivity, conditions affecting outcomes, and why water changes electrical behavior.
Visual Models

Animations and effect scenes

The game uses effects to make invisible processes easier to discuss.

Teacher link: particle models, ions, energy transfer, and visual models of unseen chemical processes.
What To Tell Students
A simple framing statement teachers can use before play begins.
This game teaches chemistry through strategic play. Some cards are direct models of real reactions, and some are simplified gameplay versions designed to help students talk about reactants, products, energy, bonding, corrosion, and reactivity. Encourage students to explain why a move worked, not just what damage it caused.
Suggested Classroom Uses
Ways a teacher can connect the game to instruction.

Before a lesson

Use the game as a hook. Ask students to predict which cards should react, then compare their ideas with what happens in play.

After a lesson

Use it as consolidation. Have students explain each move using chemistry vocabulary instead of only game vocabulary.

Partner discussion

Let students play in pairs and pause after each reaction to identify reactants, products, and evidence of chemical change.

Assessment prompt

Ask students which reactions are accurate, which are simplified, and what real-world chemistry concept each one models.
